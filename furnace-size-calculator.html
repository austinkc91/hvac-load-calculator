Why Furnace Sizing Matters

A furnace that's too large or too small for your home wastes money, reduces comfort, and shortens equipment life. Proper sizing is the single most important factor in getting a furnace that keeps you warm efficiently for 15-20 years.

Oversized Furnace Problems

Short-cycles rapidly, creating hot and cold swings. Wastes gas on every startup. Increased wear on the heat exchanger and blower motor. Higher utility bills despite having "extra" capacity.

Undersized Furnace Problems

Runs nonstop during cold snaps but can't maintain temperature. Some rooms stay cold. Excessive wear from continuous operation. Higher gas bills from marathon run times.

Right-Sized Benefits

Even heat distribution, proper run cycles, lower gas bills, and a furnace that lasts its full 15-20 year lifespan. Comfortable in every room, even on the coldest days.

Efficiency Gains

A right-sized 96% AFUE furnace outperforms an oversized 80% furnace in both comfort and operating cost. Sizing and efficiency work together.

Understanding Furnace BTU: Input vs. Output

Furnaces are rated with two BTU numbers, and understanding the difference is critical for proper sizing.

BTU Input

This is the total amount of gas energy the furnace burns per hour. A 100,000 BTU input furnace consumes 100,000 BTUs worth of natural gas each hour at full capacity. This is the bigger number you'll see on the furnace nameplate.

BTU Output

This is the actual heat delivered to your home after accounting for efficiency losses. A 100,000 BTU input furnace with 96% AFUE efficiency delivers 96,000 BTU of output heat. The output number is what matters for sizing.

Factors That Affect Furnace Sizing

Climate Zone and Design Temperature

Your heating load is determined by the temperature difference between indoors (typically 70 degrees F) and the outdoor design temperature for your area. A home in Minneapolis (design temp -13 degrees F) has a much larger heating load than the same home in Dallas (design temp 22 degrees F).

Insulation and Air Sealing

Insulation is the biggest factor after climate. A home with R-38 attic insulation and sealed ductwork might need half the furnace capacity of an identical home with R-13 attic insulation and leaky ducts. Upgrading insulation before replacing a furnace often means you can install a smaller, less expensive system.

Windows and Doors

Windows are the weakest point in your building envelope. Single-pane windows lose 10-25x more heat per square foot than an insulated wall. The number, size, and type of windows dramatically affect heating load.

Ductwork Condition

Leaky ductwork in unconditioned spaces (attics, crawlspaces) can waste 20-30% of furnace output. If your ducts are in poor condition, factor that into your calculation or plan to seal them during the furnace replacement.

Frequently Asked Questions

What size furnace do I need for a 1,500 square foot house?

A typical 1,500 sq ft home needs a furnace with 45,000-75,000 BTU output, depending on climate and insulation. In a mild climate with good insulation, 45,000 BTU may suffice. In a cold climate with average insulation, you might need 70,000+ BTU. A load calculation gives you the accurate number instead of guessing.

Should I get a high-efficiency (96% AFUE) furnace?

In cold climates where the furnace runs frequently, a 96% AFUE furnace typically pays for itself in 5-7 years through gas savings compared to an 80% AFUE model. In mild climates, the payback takes longer. However, a right-sized 96% furnace always outperforms an oversized 80% furnace in comfort and efficiency.

What's the difference between single-stage and two-stage furnaces?

Single-stage furnaces run at 100% capacity or off. Two-stage furnaces have a low fire (around 65% capacity) and a high fire (100%). Two-stage models run longer at low fire, providing more even heat, better air filtration, and quieter operation. They're especially beneficial when a furnace is slightly oversized.

Can I replace my furnace with the same size?

Not necessarily. If your current furnace was improperly sized (common), or if you've improved insulation, replaced windows, or added air sealing since it was installed, the right size may be different. Always do a load calculation before selecting a replacement furnace.

Does this calculator account for altitude?

Gas furnaces lose approximately 4% of capacity per 1,000 feet of elevation above sea level due to lower air density. Our calculator focuses on the heating load side. If you're above 2,000 feet, mention this to your HVAC contractor so they can derate the furnace output accordingly.
